## Why Markspresso is different

Markspresso is built for teams who want a polished content site without committing to a full frontend framework stack. Keep content in Markdown, layouts in HTML or CFML, and deployments as plain static files.

<div class="proof-strip">
  <article class="proof-item">
    <h3>One repo, multiple site types</h3>
    <p>Run docs, blog posts, changelogs, and launch pages from one project structure and one config file.</p>
  </article>
  <article class="proof-item">
    <h3>Fast publishing workflow</h3>
    <p>Use <code>create</code>, <code>build</code>, <code>watch</code>, and <code>serve</code> for daily work with minimal ceremony.</p>
  </article>
  <article class="proof-item">
    <h3>Flexible templates</h3>
    <p>Choose simple HTML templates, CFML-powered layouts, and reusable partials to match your team style.</p>
  </article>
  <article class="proof-item">
    <h3>Built-in publishing features</h3>
    <p>Add docs navigation, optional search, feeds, front matter metadata, and draft controls without extra packages.</p>
  </article>
</div>

## Compare the approach

<div class="compare-grid">
  <article class="compare-card">
    <h3>Markspresso workflow</h3>
    <ul>
      <li>Markdown-first editing for writers and developers.</li>
      <li>Static output with pretty URLs and collection-aware routing.</li>
      <li>Low tooling overhead and straightforward CI steps.</li>
      <li>Great fit for docs, blogs, release notes, and launch sites.</li>
    </ul>
  </article>
  <article class="compare-card">
    <h3>When heavier stacks are better</h3>
    <ul>
      <li>Complex client-side app state and SPA-style interactions.</li>
      <li>Highly custom frontend pipelines with large JS ecosystems.</li>
      <li>Advanced app platform concerns beyond content publishing.</li>
      <li>Teams already standardized on framework-specific tooling.</li>
    </ul>
  </article>
</div>
